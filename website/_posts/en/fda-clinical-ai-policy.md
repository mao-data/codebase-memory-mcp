## A Regulatory Inflection Point That Changes the Deployment Calculus

In January 2026, the FDA released updated guidance on Clinical Decision Support (CDS) software. The document's impact is more far-reaching than most have recognized.

The core change: **many generative AI tools that provide diagnostic suggestions or perform supportive tasks like medical history-taking can now reach clinics without full FDA medical device review.**

This isn't a technological breakthrough—it's a shift in regulatory philosophy.

---

## What Is CDS Software and Why Does It Matter?

"Clinical Decision Support software" covers a broad range of digital tools that help clinical staff make judgments—from drug dose calculators to AI diagnostic suggestion systems.

Before the 2026 guidance update, most AI tools providing diagnostic suggestions were classified as medical devices requiring full FDA 510(k) or De Novo review: a process taking one to three years and costing millions of dollars.

The new guidance essentially says: if an AI tool is designed to **assist physician judgment rather than replace it**, and provides sufficient transparency for physicians to independently verify recommendations, it may be exempt from full review.

---

## The Other Major Policy: Predetermined Change Control Plans

Equally important is the FDA's move toward Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCP).

Traditionally, if an approved AI medical device updated its model (retrained, adjusted parameters), it had to file a full new review application. This made continuous iteration nearly impossible—many vendors simply "froze" their models after approval.

PCCP's core: **manufacturers can submit a plan for future model updates alongside their original application. Once approved, updates within the plan's scope don't require re-submission.**

This is a massive unlock for medical AI—models can continuously learn from new data while remaining compliant.

---

## FDA AI Medical Device Milestones

From 2016 to 2026, the FDA has granted "Breakthrough Device Designation" (with priority review) to over **1,200** AI/ML-related medical devices. Over **900** AI medical devices have been cleared and are now on the market, spanning radiology, cardiology, dermatology, ophthalmology, and more.

---

## The Case for the New Policy

Supporters—primarily industry and some clinicians—make these arguments:

1. **Faster patient access**: Shorter review cycles mean patients benefit from AI-assisted diagnosis sooner
2. **Lower entry barriers**: Small medical AI startups don't need tens of millions of dollars and years to reach market
3. **Physicians remain primary**: As long as AI's role is "recommend, not decide," physicians retain full clinical control

---

## The Safety Warning

In January 2026, *STAT News* published a pointed commentary: **after the FDA's pivot, AI safety research in medicine is more important than ever.**

Core concerns:

- **Validation gaps**: Which CDS tools actually work? Under new rules, many can enter clinics without clinical validation
- **Bias and equity**: AI diagnostic tools may perform very differently across demographic groups, but lighter entry requirements make systematic evaluation harder
- **Liability**: When an exempted AI tool causes harm, who is responsible?
- **Vendor self-reporting**: Without mandatory post-market surveillance, will vendors proactively identify and report AI failures?

---

## Utah's Experiment: AI Autonomous Prescription Refills

One concrete example of the policy shift's effects: in 2026, Utah launched the US's first **AI autonomous prescription refill pilot** via the Doctronic platform.

For stable chronic disease patients (hypertension, diabetes), the AI system can automatically approve prescription renewals when specific criteria are met—without physician review each time. This dramatically reduces administrative burden, but has triggered serious public discussion: where exactly is the boundary of AI autonomous decision-making in healthcare?

---

## Conclusion: The Eternal Tension Between Speed and Safety

The FDA's new policy represents a pragmatic bet: **the benefits of regulatory loosening (accelerated innovation, improved care access) outweigh the risks (safety gaps)**, and assumes market competition and physician oversight can fill regulatory gaps.

Whether that bet is correct, clinical data over the next few years will tell. What's certain: AI's entry into clinical practice is irreversible. The regulatory framework must evolve rapidly without surrendering its safety core.
